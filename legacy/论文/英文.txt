\begin{abstract}
    Particle swarm optimization is widely used in continuous optimization because of its simple structure and few control parameters. However, standard particle swarm optimization often suffers from premature convergence when particles rapidly aggregate around local optima and continue consuming computational resources with limited search contribution. To address this problem, this paper proposes an adaptive restart particle swarm optimization algorithm based on search resource reallocation. The proposed algorithm, named ARPSO-SRR, detects stagnation and diversity loss, adaptively determines restart intensity, and combines global random regeneration with local perturbation around promising regions. The core idea is to reallocate search resources from stagnated particles to new search positions under the same function evaluation budget. In addition, an inefficient particle identification based variant is included as an ablation and comparison variant to examine whether more complex particle selection provides further benefit. Experiments are designed on twenty nine benchmark functions from the CEC2017 test suite. Mean error, standard deviation, average ranking, convergence curves, restart behavior, runtime comparison, ablation study, and nonparametric statistical tests are used for evaluation. The study focuses on whether ARPSO-SRR can improve standard particle swarm optimization and related variants while maintaining competitive performance against representative evolutionary algorithms.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
    particle swarm optimization, adaptive restart, hybrid restart, search resource reallocation, numerical optimization
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}
Particle swarm optimization (PSO) is a population based metaheuristic inspired
by collective behavior and has been widely applied to continuous optimization
problems \cite{b1}. Due to its simple structure, few control parameters, and
easy implementation, PSO has become one of the most representative swarm
intelligence algorithms. In PSO, particles move in the search space according
to their own historical experience and the best position found by the swarm.
This mechanism enables PSO to solve many black box optimization problems
without requiring gradient information.

However, standard PSO may suffer from premature convergence, especially when
particles rapidly gather around local optima and lose population diversity.
Once the swarm becomes highly concentrated, many particles may repeatedly
search similar regions and fail to provide new useful information. These
particles still consume function evaluations, but their contribution to further
improvement becomes limited. This phenomenon reduces the efficiency of the
algorithm under a fixed computational budget.

Existing PSO variants have attempted to alleviate premature convergence through
adaptive parameters, diversity maintenance, comprehensive learning, hybrid
search, or restart mechanisms \cite{b2,b3,b4}. Adaptive parameter control can
improve the balance between exploration and exploitation, while comprehensive
learning can maintain useful diversity by allowing particles to learn from
different exemplars. Restart based strategies provide another practical way to
reactivate the swarm when stagnation occurs.

Although restart mechanisms are useful, two issues remain important. First, the
restart intensity should not be fixed because different search stages require
different degrees of exploration. A strong restart may destroy useful
exploitation around promising regions, while a weak restart may be insufficient
to escape local optima. Second, restarting particles can be interpreted as
reallocating computational resources. In the later stage of PSO, some particles
may repeatedly search unpromising or redundant regions, thereby consuming
function evaluations with limited contribution. Reassigning these particles to
new positions can improve the utilization of search resources.

Based on this motivation, this paper proposes an adaptive restart particle
swarm optimization algorithm based on search resource reallocation, denoted as
ARPSO-SRR. The proposed method dynamically triggers restart operations
according to stagnation and population state, and combines global random
restart with local perturbation restart. The goal is to improve the robustness
of PSO family algorithms under a controlled restart framework while retaining
the simplicity and easy implementation of the standard PSO structure.

The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item An adaptive restart PSO algorithm based on search resource reallocation is
          proposed. The method aims to alleviate premature convergence by reallocating
          search resources from stagnated particles to new search regions.
    \item A hybrid restart strategy is designed by combining global random regeneration
          and local perturbation around promising regions. This strategy attempts to
          balance exploration and exploitation during the restart process.
    \item An inefficient particle identification based variant is investigated as an
          ablation variant to analyze whether more interpretable particle selection can
          provide additional gains over the proposed ARPSO-SRR framework.
    \item Extensive experiments on 29 benchmark functions from the CEC2017 test suite are
          conducted. The proposed method is evaluated using average ranking, convergence
          curves, restart behavior, runtime comparison, ablation study, and nonparametric
          statistical tests.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Standard PSO and Adaptive PSO}
Standard PSO updates each particle according to its personal best position and
the global best position. Let $x_i^t$ and $v_i^t$ denote the position and
velocity of particle $i$ at iteration $t$. The standard PSO update can be
written as
\begin{equation}
    v_i^{t+1}=w v_i^t+c_1 r_1(p_i^t-x_i^t)+c_2 r_2(g^t-x_i^t),
    \label{eq:pso_velocity}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    x_i^{t+1}=x_i^t+v_i^{t+1},
    \label{eq:pso_position}
\end{equation}
where $w$ is the inertia weight, $c_1$ and $c_2$ are acceleration coefficients, $r_1$ and $r_2$ are random numbers, $p_i^t$ is the personal best, and $g^t$ is the global best.

Adaptive PSO variants adjust inertia weight or learning coefficients to improve
the balance between exploration and exploitation \cite{b2}. Larger inertia
weights are often used to encourage exploration in the early search stage,
while smaller inertia weights are used to enhance exploitation in the later
stage. However, parameter adaptation alone may not be sufficient when the swarm
has already stagnated in a narrow region. In such cases, particles may continue
to update their positions, but the actual search contribution becomes weak.

\subsection{Diversity Maintenance and Comprehensive Learning}
Diversity maintenance is essential for avoiding premature convergence.
Comprehensive learning PSO (CLPSO) improves diversity by allowing particles to
learn from different exemplars rather than only from the global best \cite{b4}.
Such methods provide strong baselines for evaluating whether a new PSO variant
can preserve useful exploration ability.

Although diversity based learning mechanisms are effective, they usually modify
the learning structure of PSO. Restart based mechanisms follow another
direction: they preserve the basic PSO update framework while reinitializing
part of the population when the search becomes stagnant. This makes restart
based PSO simple to implement and suitable for combination with other PSO
variants.

\subsection{Restart Based PSO}
Restart and regeneration strategies are widely used to reactivate the swarm
when stagnation occurs. Some methods randomly regenerate particles, while
others select particles according to current fitness or stagnation status. From
the perspective of search resource allocation, restart can be regarded as a
process of transferring function evaluations from low utility particles to
potentially more useful search regions.

However, a restart strategy should avoid two extreme cases. If restart is too
frequent or too strong, the algorithm may lose useful exploitation around
promising regions. If restart is too weak or too late, the swarm may remain
trapped in local optima for a long time. Therefore, adaptive restart intensity
and hybrid restart operators are important for improving the stability of
restart based PSO.

\section{Proposed Method}\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Motivation and Overall Framework}
In the later stage of PSO, a subset of particles may repeatedly search similar
or unpromising regions without improving either the global best or their
personal best. These particles consume function evaluations but contribute
little to further progress. The proposed ARPSO-SRR treats this phenomenon as
inefficient search resource usage. Instead of only modifying velocity
parameters, ARPSO-SRR adaptively restarts part of the population and
reallocates search resources to new positions.

The core idea of ARPSO-SRR consists of three parts. First, restart is triggered
according to stagnation and population state. Second, the number of restarted
particles is adjusted adaptively rather than fixed. Third, the restart operator
combines global random regeneration and local perturbation around promising
regions. In this way, ARPSO-SRR attempts to maintain exploration ability while
preserving exploitation near promising areas. The overall framework is shown in
Fig.~\ref{fig:framework}.

\subsection{Stagnation and Diversity Detection}
Population diversity reflects the distribution state of particles in the search
space. A simple diversity measure can be defined as
\begin{equation}
    D^t=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\left\|x_i^t-\bar{x}^t\right\|_2,
    \label{eq:diversity}
\end{equation}
where $N$ is the population size and $\bar{x}^t$ is the center of the swarm at iteration $t$. A smaller $D^t$ indicates that particles are more concentrated.

Stagnation is measured by the number of consecutive iterations without global
best improvement. Let $s^t$ denote the current stagnation length. When $s^t$
exceeds a predefined threshold or when diversity becomes too low, the restart
condition is activated. This design allows the algorithm to react to both
fitness stagnation and diversity loss.

\subsection{Adaptive Restart Intensity}
A fixed restart ratio may not be suitable for all search stages. Therefore,
ARPSO-SRR adjusts the restart intensity according to the search state. The
restart ratio can be expressed as
\begin{equation}
    \rho^t=\rho_{\min}+(\rho_{\max}-\rho_{\min})Q^t,
    \label{eq:restart_ratio}
\end{equation}
where $\rho_{\min}$ and $\rho_{\max}$ are the minimum and maximum restart ratios, and $Q^t$ is a normalized restart demand indicator. A higher $Q^t$ means that the swarm is more likely to be trapped or over concentrated.

In practice, $Q^t$ can be determined by combining stagnation status and
diversity loss. When the swarm stagnates for a longer time or diversity becomes
lower, more particles are selected for restart. The number of restarted
particles is then calculated as
\begin{equation}
    m^t=\left\lceil \rho^t N \right\rceil,
    \label{eq:restart_number}
\end{equation}
where $m^t$ is the number of particles to be restarted at iteration $t$.

\subsection{Hybrid Restart Strategy}
The restart operation combines two complementary strategies. The first strategy
is global random restart, which regenerates a selected particle uniformly in
the search space:
\begin{equation}
    x_i^{t+1}=l+(u-l)\odot r,
    \label{eq:global_restart}
\end{equation}
where $l$ and $u$ are the lower and upper bounds of the search space, $r$ is a random vector sampled from a uniform distribution, and $\odot$ denotes element wise multiplication.

The second strategy is local perturbation restart, which regenerates a selected
particle around the current global best:
\begin{equation}
    x_i^{t+1}=g^t+\sigma^t (u-l)\odot z,
    \label{eq:local_restart}
\end{equation}
where $g^t$ is the current global best position, $\sigma^t$ controls the perturbation strength at iteration $t$, and $z$ is a random vector. In this paper, $\sigma^t$ is not treated as an independent fixed search operator; it is bounded by $\sigma_{\min}$ and $\sigma_{\max}$ and gradually reduced with the search progress. This setting keeps local restart broad enough in earlier stages and more exploitative in later stages. The local perturbation strategy helps the algorithm exploit promising regions, while the global random strategy introduces new exploration ability.

By combining these two restart operators, ARPSO-SRR attempts to avoid both
excessive randomization and excessive local exploitation. The selected
particles are not removed from the population. Instead, their positions are
reassigned so that subsequent function evaluations can be used more effectively
under the same computational budget.

\subsection{Algorithm Procedure}
The pseudo-code of ARPSO-SRR is given in Fig.~\ref{fig:pseudocode}. The
algorithm first performs standard PSO updates. Then, it monitors stagnation and
diversity. When the restart condition is satisfied, it calculates the adaptive
restart intensity and applies hybrid restart to selected particles.

\begin{figure}[!t]
    \small
    \begin{algorithmic}[1]
        \STATE Initialize particle positions and velocities
        \STATE Evaluate fitness values and initialize personal and global bests
        \STATE Initialize stagnation counter and diversity record
        \WHILE{termination criterion is not met}
        \STATE Update velocities according to \eqref{eq:pso_velocity}
        \STATE Update positions according to \eqref{eq:pso_position}
        \STATE Evaluate particles and update personal and global bests
        \STATE Measure population diversity and stagnation status
        \IF{restart condition is satisfied}
        \STATE Compute adaptive restart ratio using \eqref{eq:restart_ratio}
        \STATE Determine the number of particles to be restarted
        \STATE Select particles for search resource reallocation
        \STATE Apply global random restart to part of selected particles
        \STATE Apply local perturbation restart to the remaining selected particles
        \STATE Reset related stagnation records
        \ENDIF
        \ENDWHILE
        \STATE Return the best solution found
    \end{algorithmic}
    \caption{Pseudo-code of ARPSO-SRR.}
    \label{fig:pseudocode}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Computational Complexity Analysis}
For standard PSO, the position and velocity update of a population with $N$
particles in a $D$ dimensional search space has a computational complexity of
$O(ND)$ per iteration, excluding the objective function evaluation cost.
ARPSO-SRR introduces two additional operations when the restart condition is
checked: diversity measurement and restart reassignment. The diversity measure
in \eqref{eq:diversity} requires the swarm center and the distance from each
particle to the center, which is also $O(ND)$. The hybrid restart operation
modifies at most $N$ particles, and therefore its worst case complexity is
$O(ND)$. Consequently, ARPSO-SRR does not change the asymptotic computational
order of standard PSO. Its additional cost mainly appears as a small constant
factor, while the total number of function evaluations remains unchanged.

\section{Experimental Setup}\label{sec:experiment}

\subsection{Benchmark Functions}
The experiments are conducted on 29 benchmark functions from the CEC2017 test
suite, namely F1 and F3--F30 \cite{b5}. Following common practice, F2 is
excluded. All experiments are conducted in 30 dimensions. The maximum number of
function evaluations is set to $10000 \times D$, where $D=30$.

The CEC2017 benchmark contains different types of functions, including
unimodal, multimodal, hybrid, and composition functions. These functions are
suitable for evaluating not only final optimization accuracy but also the
robustness of algorithms under different landscape characteristics. Therefore,
the benchmark is used as the main test suite in this paper.

\subsection{Compared Algorithms}
The proposed ARPSO-SRR is compared with standard PSO and several representative
algorithms: PSO with adaptive inertia weight, PSO with restart strategy, CLPSO,
HPSO-TVAC, genetic algorithm (GA), differential evolution (DE), and ARPSO-SRR
\cite{b6,b7}. An EIS based variant, denoted as ARPSO-EIS, is also included in
the ablation study and overall comparison.

ARPSO-EIS uses a more explicit particle selection rule for restart. It
considers fitness disadvantage, spatial crowding, and historical contribution
to score candidate particles. This variant is used only as an ablation and
comparison variant. Its purpose is to examine whether more complex and
interpretable particle selection can provide additional gains over the simpler
ARPSO-SRR framework.

The optimization error is computed as
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{Error}=f(x_{best})-f(x^*),
    \label{eq:error}

where $x_{best}$ is the best solution found by an algorithm and $x^*$ is the known optimum. Mean error, standard deviation, average ranking, convergence curve, runtime, and restart count are reported. Wilcoxon signed-rank test and Friedman test are used to evaluate statistical significance \cite{b8,b9,b10}.

For each compared method, the same stopping criterion is used to ensure a fair
comparison. Since the proposed method changes the restart behavior rather than
the evaluation budget, no additional function evaluations are introduced. This
setting is important because the improvement should come from better resource
allocation inside the swarm rather than from a larger computational budget.

restart framework before comparing the complete method with all baseline
algorithms. Several restart variants are compared under the same population
size, maximum function evaluations, benchmark functions, and random seed
generation rule. The purpose is to examine whether adaptive restart intensity
and hybrid restart improve the basic restart framework.

In addition, ARPSO-EIS is included to examine whether more complex particle
selection provides stable extra performance gains. If ARPSO-EIS performs
similarly to ARPSO-SRR, the result suggests that EIS improves interpretability
but does not necessarily guarantee additional optimization performance. In this
case, ARPSO-SRR achieves a better balance between computational simplicity and

The results should be discussed in a confident but conservative manner.
Compared with DE and CLPSO, ARPSO-SRR does not aim to replace their specialized
search mechanisms. Instead, it provides a simple restart based enhancement for
the PSO family. If the final results show competitive behavior on multimodal,
hybrid, or composition functions, this can be attributed to the adaptive
restart intensity and the combination of global and local restart operations.
If ARPSO-EIS obtains comparable but not consistently better performance than
ARPSO-SRR, it indicates that a more interpretable particle selection mechanism
does not necessarily lead to stable additional performance gains when the
adaptive hybrid restart mechanism is already effective. Thus, ARPSO-SRR
achieves a better balance between computational simplicity and search
effectiveness compared with the more complex ARPSO-EIS.

The expected advantage of ARPSO-SRR is most meaningful on functions where
premature convergence and diversity loss are more likely to occur. On simple
functions, frequent restart may not always provide an obvious benefit because
the standard swarm can already approach good solutions. On multimodal, hybrid,
and composition functions, adaptive restart can help the swarm leave over
concentrated regions and continue exploring alternative areas. Therefore, the
final discussion should analyze results by function type rather than relying
only on the overall average rank.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
This paper proposed ARPSO-SRR, an adaptive restart PSO algorithm based on
search resource reallocation. The proposed method adjusts restart intensity
according to the search state and combines global random restart with local
perturbation restart to balance exploration and exploitation. Experiments on
CEC2017 benchmark functions are designed to evaluate the effectiveness of
ARPSO-SRR.

The experimental analysis is expected to show that ARPSO-SRR can stably improve
standard PSO and several PSO family variants. In addition, the EIS based
extension variant is investigated to analyze whether more interpretable
particle selection can provide further improvement. The results should be
discussed objectively: EIS may improve interpretability, but it does not
necessarily bring stable additional performance gains over ARPSO-SRR. Future
work will investigate more adaptive parameter control, larger scale benchmarks,
and real world optimization applications.
